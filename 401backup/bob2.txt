## Source 2 — Import AI (Jack Clark) — Issue 451, March 30, 2026

- **Political Superintelligence.** Stanford political economy professor Andy Hall argues that AI could give every person on the planet access to a "political superintelligence" — tools helping citizens, representatives, and institutions perceive reality more sharply, understand tradeoffs, and act more effectively in democratic processes. Clark notes that getting there requires significant work on the UX, governance, and oversight of those systems, not just the AI itself.

- **Google's "Society of Minds" Paper.** Google researchers argue that the next intelligence explosion won't come from a single monolithic AI, but from cooperative, competitive, and creative interaction among multitudes of socially intelligent minds — most of them non-biological. They draw on the history of human institutions (writing, law, markets) as blueprints for designing AI-centric governance.

- **Hyperagents: Meta-Backed Self-Improving AI.** Researchers from UBC, NYU, Edinburgh, and Meta built "Darwin Godel Machine Hyperagents" — a system where an LLM can iteratively improve both its own task-solving prompts and the mechanism it uses to generate future improvements, working across generations like an evolutionary process. Results showed dramatic performance gains on coding, paper review, robotics reward design, and olympiad-level math.

- **HorizonMath: A Contamination-Proof Math Benchmark.** Researchers from Oxford, Harvard, Princeton, and the Ellison Institute built HorizonMath — 100 problems in applied and computational mathematics that are "predominantly unsolved," meaning they can't exist in any training corpus. The highest-scoring model, GPT 5.4 Pro, solved only 7% of the full dataset.

- **UK Government Finds Scaling Law for AI Cyberattacks.** The UK AI Security Institute tested frontier models on simulated multi-step cyberattack scenarios and found that each successive model generation outperforms its predecessor, with the best single run completing 22 of 32 steps on a corporate network attack chain. Increasing inference-time compute yielded gains of up to 59%.
