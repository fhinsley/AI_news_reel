ARC-AGI-3 draws a new line in the sand

<break time="1s" />

The ARC Prize Foundation launched ARC-AGI-3 this week at Y Combinator in San Francisco, and the benchmark's opening results have reframed the conversation about where frontier AI actually stands. Unlike its predecessors, which presented AI systems with static grid puzzles, ARC-AGI-3 drops agents into turn-based game environments with no rules, no stated goals, and no instructions. To score, an agent must explore the environment, infer what winning looks like, and carry that learning forward across increasingly difficult levels. Humans, with no special training, solved all one hundred thirty-five environments. Every tested frontier model, including Gemini 3.1, GPT 5.4, and Claude Opus 4.6, scored below one percent.

<break time="1s" />

The benchmark's creator, Francois Chollet, was direct about what the results mean. Chollet argues that when ordinary humans can handle the same tasks without instructions or tools, there is no reason AGI should need special handholding and custom prompts, since the G in AGI stands for general. The ARC Prize Foundation is running ARC Prize 2026 through Kaggle with more than two million dollars in prizes, and the competition runs through November. Notably, the top scoring system during a developer preview phase was not a large language model at all. Explicit graph search, state tracking, and systematic exploration outperformed every frontier language model by a substantial margin, with a CNN-based agent doing structured exploration beating the best GPT-series agents by over twelve percentage points.

Qwen3.5 challenges models ten times its size

<break time="1s" />

Alibaba released its Qwen3.5 family of open-weights vision-language models this week, and the results across benchmark tests are drawing attention. The family includes impressive larger models as well as a smaller one that outperforms an OpenAI open-weights model ten times its size. The models use a mixture-of-experts architecture and span sizes from less than one billion parameters up through flagship scale, giving developers meaningful options at both the edge and the cloud. Qwen3.5 is the latest in a series of open-weights releases from Chinese technology companies that have consistently challenged the assumption that competitive model performance requires access to high-end American hardware.

<break time="1s" />

Hyperagents learn to improve themselves

<break time="1s" />

Researchers from the University of British Columbia, NYU, the University of Edinburgh, the Vector Institute, CIFAR, and Meta published a paper this week describing what they call a hyperagent, a self-referential system that can iteratively improve both its own task-solving behavior and the mechanism that generates future improvements. As Jack Clark of Import AI described it, the approach means giving a language model a scaffold it can edit, then stepping back and letting successive generations of improvement run until performance saturates.

<break time="1s" />

The system, formally named the Darwin Godel Machine Hyperagent, was tested across four domains including coding, paper review, robotics reward design, and olympiad-level math grading, using Claude Sonnet 4.5 as the base model for most tasks. In all cases, the hyperagent approach improved performance significantly across evaluations conducted by several different models including o3-mini, GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4.5, and o4-mini. The research represents a step toward AI systems that do not simply perform tasks but actively improve the way they approach tasks, compounding capability across generations without requiring additional human intervention.

Google frames AI alignment as a social problem

<break time="1s" />

A paper from Google researchers published this week argues that the dominant framing of AI alignment, which focuses on making a single model safe and controllable, misses the larger challenge ahead. The researchers argue that the future of intelligence is less about building a monolithic singleton that runs the world and more about figuring out how to build institutions capable of dealing with a vast proliferation of AI agents working in tandem with humans.

<break time="1s" />

The paper draws on the history of prior intelligence explosions, from primate social groups to human language to writing and bureaucracy, to argue that each leap was driven by new forms of social coordination rather than individual cognitive hardware upgrades. The researchers write that the path to more powerful AI runs not through building a single colossal oracle but through composing richer social systems, and that these systems will be hybrid ones combining AI and humans. They argue that governments will need AI systems with distinct, explicitly embedded values such as transparency, equity, and due process, whose function is to check and balance other AI systems deployed in the private sector. The paper is being presented at ICLR 2026 in April.

Apple unifies visual tokenization across media types

<break time="1s" />

Apple researchers published work this week describing AToken, a multimodal architecture that uses a single tokenizer to encode images, videos, and three-dimensional objects, rather than relying on separate encoders for each modality. Most multimodal models use different tokenizers to embed different media types, and different encoders when training to generate media rather than classify it. Apple's approach unifies this pipeline, which could simplify the training and deployment of multimodal systems and make it easier to reason across different visual formats within a single model. The research extends a pattern of Apple publishing work in AI architecture that informs future product capabilities without announcing specific product timelines.

Model Context Protocol crosses ninety-seven million installs

<break time="1s" />

The Model Context Protocol crossed ninety-seven million installs in March 2026, signaling its transition from an experimental standard to foundational agentic infrastructure. Every major AI provider now ships MCP-compatible tooling. MCP, which Anthropic introduced in late 2024, defines a standard interface for AI models to interact with external tools, data sources, and services. The install milestone, published this week, positions MCP alongside other infrastructure-layer standards and has prompted comparisons to the role HTTP played in standardizing communication across the web. Enterprise developers have embraced the protocol as a way to build model-agnostic integrations that do not require rewriting tool connectors each time a team switches foundation models.

Waymo reaches five hundred thousand rides per week

Waymo is now providing five hundred thousand paid robotaxi rides every week across ten U.S. cities, a tenfold increase from fifty thousand weekly rides in May 2024, a period of less than two years. The Alphabet-owned company expanded from its original markets in Phoenix, San Francisco, and Los Angeles to seven additional Sun Belt cities over the past year, including Austin, Atlanta, Miami, Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, and Orlando. Waymo raised a sixteen billion dollar investment round earlier this year, valuing the company at one hundred twenty-six billion dollars.

Waymo is targeting one million rides per week by the end of the year, according to the company's co-CEO, and lists around twenty additional cities as next markets, including Tokyo and London. The growth has come with increased regulatory attention. AI governance debate accelerates at state level

<break time="1s" />

With federal AI legislation stalled in Congress, a growing number of U.S. states have moved forward with their own AI regulations, creating a patchwork of requirements that is increasingly drawing pushback from the White House. In the absence of action at the federal level, states have been passing dozens of their own laws regulating artificial intelligence, including guidelines for child safety, requirements for greater transparency from developers, and whistleblower protections. The White House and its AI policy advisors have argued that state-by-state rules create uncertainty that burdens innovation, with the head of the White House Office of Science and Technology stating that providing developers with consistent national standards is something only Congress can accomplish. State lawmakers in both parties are navigating competing pressures from industry, from constituents, and from federal officials pushing for a federal-first approach.

Toward political superintelligence

<break time="1s" />

A widely discussed paper published this week by Stanford political economy professor Andy Hall, and highlighted in Jack Clark's Import AI newsletter, argues that AI systems could become a transformative force in how citizens, legislators, and institutions engage with policy. Hall defines political superintelligence not as a single all-powerful AI, but as a layered set of tools that gives individuals and governments the ability to perceive reality more sharply, understand tradeoffs, contest power, and act more effectively.

<break time="1s" />

Hall outlines three distinct layers: an information layer in which AI changes how governments access data and hear from citizens, a representation layer in which AI delegates could monitor politics on behalf of individuals and suggest or even execute policy actions, and a governance layer that determines who controls the underlying infrastructure and under what rules. The paper's central argument is that while getting to political superintelligence requires advances in the AI itself, the harder challenge is building the institutional structures, oversight mechanisms, and participatory interfaces that ensure the resulting power is distributed rather than concentrated. Hall writes that he is not interested in slowing AI development, but in speeding up the construction of the structures that keep democratic societies free as AI becomes more powerful.
